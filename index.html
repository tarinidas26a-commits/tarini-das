My Story

Not a straight line.
A deliberate one.

From blueprints to boardrooms — every detour was actually a shortcut.

I started as someone who wanted to build things — spaces, structures, systems. Architecture was not just a degree; it was a way of thinking. I learned to read the relationship between form and function, between a blueprint and the humans who would eventually live inside it.

During an internship, I discovered something unexpected: people are the most complex structure of all. No blueprint. No load-bearing logic. And yet organisations depend on getting that architecture right every single day. I changed paths — and I have never looked back.

What most people called a detour, I call my sharpest edge. Every domain I have crossed — architecture, recruitment, L&D, compliance, analytics — added a layer that a straight HR career path simply could not have given me.

I walked into an HR function that ran on instinct and spreadsheets. I built dashboards, redesigned frameworks, and introduced data where decisions were previously made by gut. I became the analytics person in a non-analytics environment — and I realised that is exactly the kind of person most organisations desperately need.

Now I am building toward something bigger: where workforce strategy meets data intelligence — and where HR finally gets to sit at the table it has always deserved.

Diagnostic Analytics · Power BI · IBM Dataset
HR Workforce Analytics Dashboard
Problem

Most organisations know how many people they have. Very few know what those people are actually costing them — and why. The IBM HR dataset had 1,470 employee records but no narrative. The question I set was simple: if a CHRO walked in tomorrow and asked "where is our biggest people risk right now?" — could the data answer that?

Approach

I built a 6-page Power BI dashboard structured around five executive questions — not just charts. Every page had one anchor insight that a non-HR leader could act on. Custom DAX measures for attrition rate, replacement cost (SHRM 1.5x methodology), flight risk score, burnout risk flag, and a composite Workforce Risk Index. Every KPI benchmarked against industry standard. The Risk Radar page combined overtime + no promotion in 3 years + low satisfaction into a single flight risk flag.

Outcome

Attrition rate of 16.1% against a 13% benchmark. Estimated $9.25M replacement cost. Employees on overtime 3x more likely to leave. Women dropped below 30% at senior levels alongside an 8.2% unexplained gender pay gap. 36 employees flagged as active flight risks. These are findings a CHRO would walk into a board meeting with.

Reflections
"This was the project where I stopped thinking like an HR person filling in a dashboard and started thinking like a consultant building a business case. The biggest learning: data without a benchmark is just noise — every number needs a reference point to become a finding."

Cost Intelligence · Power BI · Nexova Technologies
Workforce Cost Intelligence Dashboard
Problem

Nexova Technologies had workforce data but no visibility into whether they were getting returns on their people investment. Headcount was tracked. Salaries were paid. But no one had connected absenteeism to disengagement, or overtime to productivity drain, or attrition cost to department-level budget impact. The CFO question — "what is our workforce actually costing us, beyond payroll?" — had no answer.

Approach

A 4-page Power BI dashboard built around workforce cost intelligence — not basic HR metrics. Page 1 mapped total workforce cost by department. Page 2 used absenteeism and late-day patterns as leading indicators of disengagement. Page 3 created a human capital ROI metric — salary per performance point. Page 4 built a full attrition replacement cost model by department using SHRM's 1.5x methodology.

Outcome

Overall attrition at 33.44% — more than double the 15% benchmark. Total estimated replacement cost: Rs. 10.25 million. Production accounted for 80% of all exits. Sales showed highest absenteeism and lowest satisfaction simultaneously — a compounding risk signal. Each finding framed as a CFO-level business conversation, not an HR report.

Tech Stack
Power BI DesktopDAXKaggle HR DatasetSHRM MethodologyExecutive Layout
Reflections
"Saying 'attrition is high' is an HR observation. Saying 'your attrition is costing Rs. 10.25M in replacement cost, concentrated in one department' is a CFO conversation. That reframe — from HR metric to business cost — is what makes People Analytics credible in the boardroom."

HR Transformation Roadmap — NovaPay
Problem

NovaPay is a Series C fintech scaling from 300 to 800 employees in 18 months. At that growth rate, the HR function they have will break. No HRBP structure. No scalable hiring infrastructure. No HRIS capable of handling 2.6x headcount. The business problem was not "hire more people" — it was: how do you build the HR infrastructure before the growth exposes the gaps?

Approach

Started with a current-state diagnosis — mapping what a 300-person fintech HR function looks like and where pressure points emerge at scale. Designed the future-state model using the Ulrich 3-Pillar framework (COE, HRBP, Shared Services) calibrated to 800 headcount. Recommended a specific tech stack — Workday, Greenhouse, Culture Amp, Power BI — justified against growth stage and compliance requirements. The 90-day roadmap broken into four phases: stabilise, structure, scale, sustain.

Outcome

A 14-slide consulting-grade HR strategy deck that a CHRO or Series C Board could present to investors or use as an internal transformation blueprint. Every recommendation tied to a business outcome. 6 KPIs defined to make the strategy accountable — not aspirational.

Case Study · Real Work · Anonymised
Building HR Analytics Infrastructure from Scratch
Situation

I joined an educational institution with 130+ employees and 4 colleges as the sole HR Manager and HRBP. The HR function had no data infrastructure — no dashboards, no structured reporting, no way for senior leadership to make data-informed people decisions. All workforce information lived in disconnected spreadsheets with no common logic.

Approach

I began with a data audit — mapping what data existed, where it lived, and what leadership actually needed to know. I identified 7 critical KPIs: headcount, attrition, productivity indicators, DEIB metrics, L&D effectiveness, policy compliance, and engagement proxies. I then built 4 integrated Power BI dashboards — one per strategic question — consolidating all data into a single reporting layer and creating a consistent data language across HR and leadership.

Outcome

All 4 dashboards were adopted into weekly senior leadership review within the first quarter. Workforce data was being used to drive conversations — not just track headcount. Leadership could see attrition signals, training effectiveness, and workforce composition at a glance. This became the foundation for every subsequent strategic HR conversation at the institution.

Reflections
"The hardest part was not the technical build — it was getting people to trust the data. HR analytics only works when the business sees it as their tool, not HR's report. That shift in perception was the real deliverable."

Research Project · 2026 · Ongoing
Emotional Intelligence as a Catalyst in Skill Development
Purpose

This research investigates the role of emotional intelligence in accelerating professional skill development among working individuals in suburban Mumbai. The premise: EI is not a soft skill — it is a cognitive multiplier that shapes how quickly and deeply people acquire new capabilities in the workplace.

Approach

Primary research using structured surveys and interviews across working professionals in suburban Mumbai. Analysis correlates EI competency scores with self-reported and manager-reported skill development velocity across different domains and industries.

Expected Outcome

Findings will inform how L&D programs can be redesigned to develop emotional intelligence as a prerequisite for skill acceleration — not as a separate module, but as the foundation layer of any capability-building initiative.

Status
"This research sits at the intersection of two things I care deeply about — human potential and data-driven L&D design. Results expected mid-2026."
